# Positioning

How to define what your product is, who it's for, and why it wins. Positioning is not marketing fluff - it's the foundation that makes everything else work.

---

## Five-Component Positioning (April Dunford)

Positioning is context. It's the frame that defines how people perceive your product. Change the frame, change the perception. Get it wrong and nothing downstream - messaging, sales, product decisions - will land.

### The Five Components (In Order)

**1. Competitive Alternatives**
What would customers use if you didn't exist? This is your real competition - and it's often not who you think.

- Don't start with "our competitors are X and Y." Start with "what are customers actually doing today to solve this problem?"
- Alternatives include: doing nothing, manual processes, spreadsheets, hiring someone, building internally, using a product in a completely different category
- "We compete with Jira" might be wrong. You might compete with "a shared spreadsheet and a weekly standup."

**2. Unique Attributes**
What do you have that the alternatives don't? These must be facts, not claims.

- Features, capabilities, technical architecture, team expertise, data, integrations, pricing model
- Be honest. If three competitors have the same feature, it's not unique.
- Focus on what's defensibly different, not what's marginally better

**3. Value (That Attributes Enable)**
Features aren't value. Value is what those features enable for the customer.

- "Real-time collaboration" (attribute) enables "your team can work on specs simultaneously without merge conflicts" (value)
- Always translate: "We have X" becomes "which means you can Y"
- The value must matter to the customer. If they don't care about the problem your attribute solves, it's not value - it's trivia.

**4. Target Customers**
Who cares most about the value you deliver? Position for them, not the broadest possible audience.

- Best-fit customers: people who get the most value from your unique strengths
- They buy faster, complain less, churn less, and refer more
- "Everyone who manages projects" is not a target. "Technical PMs at mid-stage startups who need engineering buy-in" is.
- It's OK to narrow the target. Owning a niche beats being forgettable in a crowd.

**5. Market Category**
Which category should you position in? This triggers a set of assumptions in the buyer's mind.

- Choosing "project management" triggers different expectations than "engineering workflow" or "product operations"
- Pick a category where your strengths are obvious advantages
- If you're creating a new category, know that it's expensive and slow. Only do it if no existing category serves you.
- You can change categories. "Repositioning" from one category to another can transform a struggling product into a winner.

### Positioning Process

1. Start with your best customers - the ones who love you. Understand why.
2. Map their alternatives. What were they using before? What would they switch to?
3. Identify your unique attributes relative to those specific alternatives.
4. Translate attributes into value that those specific customers care about.
5. Define the target customer profile based on who gets the most value.
6. Choose the market category that makes your value obvious.

### Positioning Pitfalls

- **Positioning by aspiration**: "We're the AI-powered future of work" - what does that mean to a buyer making a purchase decision today?
- **Positioning by feature**: "We have Gantt charts, Kanban boards, and time tracking" - so does everyone else.
- **Positioning by competitor**: "We're like Slack but for X" - this makes Slack the reference point, not you.
- **Positioning for everyone**: "For teams of all sizes" - this is positioning for nobody.

### Positioning Is a Team Sport

Don't do this alone. Cross-functional input (sales, support, engineering, marketing) catches blind spots:
- Sales knows what alternatives come up in deals
- Support knows what customers struggle with
- Engineering knows what's actually unique technically
- Marketing knows how the category is perceived

## Smallest Viable Audience (Seth Godin)

Marketing isn't about shouting louder. It's about finding the specific people who need what you offer and delighting them completely.

### Core Concept

Don't try to reach everyone. Find the smallest group of people who would be devastated if your product disappeared. Serve them so well that they can't help but tell others.

- "Who is this for?" is the most important question. If the answer is "everyone," you haven't done the work.
- The smallest viable audience is big enough to sustain the business but small enough to truly serve.
- Paradox: narrowing the audience often increases growth because word-of-mouth from delighted customers beats broad mediocre marketing.

### Identity Marketing

People don't buy products. They buy the story about who they become. "People like us do things like this."

- **Affiliation**: Some people are motivated by fitting in. They want to do what respected peers do.
- **Dominance**: Others are motivated by standing out. They want to be first, best, or different.
- Understand which drives your audience and frame accordingly.

### Tension and the Status Quo

Marketing works by creating tension between where people are and where they could be, then offering a path forward.

- The status quo is comfortable. Change requires motivation.
- The tension must be real, not manufactured. "You're losing 40% of assignments" creates real tension. "Unlock your team's potential" doesn't.
- Your product resolves the tension. The promise: "things can be better, and here's how."

### Permission Marketing

Earn attention rather than stealing it. Build an audience that wants to hear from you.

- The privilege of delivering anticipated, personal, relevant messages to people who want them
- Interruption marketing (ads, spam, cold outreach) has diminishing returns
- Permission must be earned and maintained. Every communication either builds or burns trust.

### Empathy Over Sympathy

See the world as your customer sees it. Don't project your worldview onto them.

- Empathy: "I understand why you're frustrated with this workflow because I've seen what it costs you"
- Sympathy: "That sounds really hard" (from a distance, without understanding)
- The best positioning comes from genuinely understanding the customer's world - their constraints, their politics, their fears, their aspirations

## Positioning in Practice

### For Product Decisions

Every feature decision is a positioning decision. "Should we build enterprise SSO?" is really "are we positioning for enterprise buyers?" The feature shapes who you attract and who you repel.

### For Messaging

Your homepage, your pitch deck, your product tours - all positioning. Test by asking: "Would my best-fit customer read this and think 'this is exactly for me'? Would a bad-fit customer read it and self-select out?"

### For Competitive Response

When a competitor launches a feature you don't have, don't panic. Ask: "Does this matter to our best-fit customers?" If yes, you may need to respond. If no, it's noise. Positioning gives you the filter.

### The Repositioning Signal

You need to reposition when:
- Sales cycles are long because buyers don't understand what you are
- You keep losing to competitors you think you're better than
- Customers describe you differently than you describe yourself
- Your best customers use you for something different than what you designed for
- "What do you do?" requires a five-minute explanation

## Critiquing this artifact

Use this checklist when reviewing a positioning document, positioning one-pager, or messaging framework. Apply it in order - each component builds on the previous one.

### What a strong positioning document must contain

- [ ] **Competitive alternatives identified from the customer's perspective.** The document names what customers actually do today when the product does not exist. Spreadsheets, manual processes, nothing, a different category of tool. Naming only direct SaaS competitors is incomplete.
- [ ] **Unique attributes are genuinely unique.** Each stated attribute is absent from the competitive alternatives. If two or more competitors can make the same claim, the attribute is not unique - remove it or find the real differentiator.
- [ ] **Value is translated from attributes.** For each unique attribute, the document explicitly states what it enables for the customer. Attributes are facts; value is what those facts mean for the customer's work. A positioning doc that lists features without translation has stopped halfway.
- [ ] **Target customer is specific.** Names a job, seniority level, company stage, or context narrow enough that a specific person could read it and say "that is me." "Teams of all sizes" or "anyone who manages projects" is not a target.
- [ ] **Market category is chosen deliberately.** The document explains which category the product is placed in and why that category makes the value obvious to the target customer. If the category is new, the document acknowledges the cost of category creation.
- [ ] **The five components are internally consistent.** The target customer, their alternatives, the unique attributes, the value, and the category all point at the same customer situation. Any component that contradicts another must be reconciled.

### Checklist for the positioning one-pager specifically

- [ ] A best-fit customer could read the headline and self-identify within five seconds
- [ ] A bad-fit customer could read it and self-select out (positioning for everyone is positioning for no one)
- [ ] The "why us over X" question is answerable from the document without additional context
- [ ] No category hedging ("we are a cross between X and Y") - a specific category is named

### Common failure modes

**Differentiation failures** - the positioning is not actually differentiated:
- Unique attributes include "ease of use", "great customer support", "best-in-class UX" - these are claims, not facts, and every competitor makes them
- Value statements are generic benefits that apply to every product in the category ("save time", "reduce friction", "improve collaboration")
- Target customer is defined so broadly that the positioning cannot be tested against a real person

**Structural failures** - the five-component logic breaks down:
- Alternatives are listed as SaaS competitors only, ignoring non-consumption and manual processes
- Attributes are listed without translation to value - the reader must infer the "so what"
- The cascade has gaps: attributes that do not connect to stated value, or value claims with no supporting attribute
- Category choice is not explained - the document names a category without explaining why it was chosen over alternatives

**Completeness failures** - sections missing or underdeveloped:
- No market category named at all ("we are a new kind of tool")
- Positioning was not tested with sales or support to check whether it matches the language of actual deals
- No mention of repositioning signals - the document has no mechanism for knowing when the positioning needs to change

**The sales reality test**: ask whether the stated positioning matches the language that closes deals. If sales consistently uses different framing when positioning in live calls, the document does not reflect real positioning.
